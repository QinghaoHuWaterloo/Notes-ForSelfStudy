\subsection{Introduction}
Proof by contradiction method is not limited to proving just conditional statements - it can be used to prove any kind of statement. 
The basic idea is to assume that the statement we want to prove is false, and then show that this assumption leads to nonsense. 
we are then lead to conclude that we were wrong to assume the statement was false, so the statement must be true. 

Given statement $P$, let us work on the outline for Proof by Contradiction. 
\begin{proposition}
    P
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
    Suppose $\neg P$. 

    $\cdots$

    $\cdots$

    Therefore $C \wedge \neg C$.
\end{proof}

\begin{definition}
    [Rational and Irrational]
    A real number x is rational if $x = \tfrac{a}{b}$ for some $a, b \in \Z$.
    Also, x is irrational if it is not rational, that is if $x \neq \tfrac{a}{b}$ for every $a, b \in \Z$.
\end{definition}

\subsection{Proving Conditional Statements by Contradiction}
Given statement $P \Rightarrow Q$, we need to prove this statement by contradiction. 
We begin with assumption that $\neg \left(P \Rightarrow Q\right)$ is true, that is, that $P \Rightarrow Q$ is false. 
We know that $\neg (P \Rightarrow Q) \equiv (P \wedge \neg Q)$. So the first step in the proof is to assume $P$ and $\neg Q$. 

\subsubsection*{Outline for proving a conditional statement with contradiction}
\begin{proposition}
    $P \Rightarrow Q$
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
    Suppose $P$ and $\neg Q$.

    $\cdots$

    Therefore $(C \wedge \neg C)$.
\end{proof}
